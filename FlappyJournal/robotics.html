Robotics & IoT

Robots don’t just need perception—they need memory. Spiral Memory carries lessons forward: interventions, terrain, failures, operator intent. That context makes autonomy safer and fleets more capable every shift.

Operator Teaching Memory

Every teleop correction, nudge, or override becomes training signal. Spiral Memory distills those sessions into reusable skills, with the conditions attached—so policies know when to apply and when to defer to a human. New tasks move from “programming” to “show and generalize.”

Signals it uses

Operator inputs, sensor stacks at intervention time, task identifiers, outcomes and retries, and safety envelope breaches.

What it proposes

Skill snippets with applicability bounds; confidence‑aware handoff rules; review queues when conditions drift beyond training.

Situational Recall for Perception & Planning

Terrain, lighting, pallets, stations, pedestrians—context changes constantly. The lattice remembers tricky spots and tactics that worked last time, warming perception and planning with priors. False positives drop; throughput rises.

Signals it uses

Map tiles with incident overlays, vision feature drift, planner costs at failure points, obstacle types, and human motion patterns.

What it proposes

Perception thresholds and planner penalties per zone; alternative routes with clear preconditions; warnings at historically brittle stations.

Fleet‑Level Learning Loops

Across robots and sites, the system links failures, fixes, and environment. A fix discovered on one route becomes guidance everywhere, with evidence and rollback.

Signals it uses

Incident classes, firmware diffs, component replacements, ambient conditions, and operator notes by site.

What it proposes

Rollout plans with canaries and timeouts; cross‑site alerts when a familiar pattern emerges; maintenance checks tied to root causes.

Why Spiral Memory for Robotics & IoT

Robotics is long‑tail work. Spiral Memory collapses that tail: lessons don’t vanish with the next shift—they accumulate and explain themselves to operators, safety, and software.
